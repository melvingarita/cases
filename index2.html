Flagship Case
BN Valores · CEO · 2018–2023

Building an Institutional Benchmark

From a 71-person subsidiary without formal strategic planning to a nationally and internationally recognized model of institutional excellence.

The Institutional Challenge

When I was appointed CEO, BN Valores operated without a formal strategy execution system. Strategic decisions were concentrated in three people. Execution was disconnected from intent, and financial performance reflected it — an efficiency ratio above 1.0 meant the subsidiary was spending more than it earned.

The Hard Decisions

I restructured the leadership team within the first 90 days — a move that generated significant internal resistance in a public-sector context. I redesigned incentives to link individual performance to strategic objectives, accepting that cultural pushback would precede results. I also committed to external benchmarks (McKinsey OHI and the International Best Practice Competition) as irreversible accountability mechanisms. Throughout, discipline had to be maintained amid regulatory pressure and market volatility.

What I Built

A strategy execution system cascaded to every team. Seventy-one people with defined objectives, indicators, targets, and accountability. A Balanced Scorecard that connected board intent to front-line delivery.

The Results

ROAE10.3% → 16.0% (+56%)
Efficiency Ratio1.13 → 0.77
NPS~2x world-class benchmark
McKinsey OHITop Decile — LATAM
National Excellence Award2020
IBPC2022

“In 2020, BN Valores was awarded the National Excellence Award after demonstrating outstanding and well-balanced performance across all critical dimensions of its management model: leadership and strategic planning, customers and markets, human talent, processes, and innovation and technology.”

— Carlos Montenegro, Executive Director, Costa Rican Chamber of Industries

“I was very impressed by BN Valores’ well-structured approach to linking strategy with day-to-day operations. It is a great best practice to learn from, particularly because it turns strategic priorities into clear actions and measures results across social, economic, and environmental outcomes.”

— Dr. Robin Mann, Chairman, International Best Practice Competition

What I Learned

Building an institution is harder than rescuing one. Sustained performance requires holding standards when urgency fades and the temptation to declare victory appears.

Banco Nacional de Costa Rica · Deputy General Manager, Operations · 2024–Present

Institutional Discipline at Systemic Scale

Overseeing 1,700+ professionals, a ~USD 160 million operating budget, and the execution infrastructure of one of the largest banks in Central America (~USD 17B in assets).

The Institutional Challenge

Banco Nacional’s operations serve millions of customers across one of Central America’s largest financial institutions. The issue was not failure, but the lack of systematic connection between strategic objectives and daily execution. Efficiency, fraud mitigation, revenue generation, and service quality operated as parallel priorities rather than integrated dimensions of one system.

The Hard Decisions

I introduced a strategy execution architecture designed to connect institutional objectives with operational delivery at a significantly larger scale. The challenge was no longer alignment across 71 people, but across more than 1,700. This required confronting entrenched routines, renegotiating priorities across units, and making tradeoffs between short-term cost control and long-term capability building — decisions whose full consequences would unfold over years.

What I Built

A unified strategy execution system linking institutional objectives to operational delivery. Strategic maps cascaded to every team. Performance indicators with clear owners and accountability.

The Results

Process cycle times-52%
Fraud-related losses-41%
Fee income vs. target+25% above target
Overtime costs-53%
NPS1.15x above benchmark
SLA compliance100%

What I Learned

At this scale, the system must carry the discipline that personal oversight cannot. The leader’s role shifts from directing execution to ensuring the architecture holds under its own weight.

Banco Nacional de Costa Rica · Head of Trading Desks · 2016–2018

Capital Allocation and Market Judgment

Leading approximately one-third of the bank’s intermediation economics — ~USD 218 million annually — through sovereign volatility and structural market uncertainty.

The Institutional Challenge

The role carried direct responsibility for a material portion of the bank’s intermediation revenue. Every position involved balance sheet exposure, counterparty risk, and regulatory implications in a market with shallow liquidity and concentrated risks. This was capital allocation under persistent uncertainty — shaped by sovereign risk, currency volatility, shallow liquidity, and concentrated counterparties.

The Hard Decisions

Judgment was exercised daily under incomplete information. Pricing models from developed markets required adaptation. I balanced return optimization against exposure limits, managed duration through rating transitions, and at times chose not to take positions that, while attractive, did not align with institutional risk parameters.

What I Built

Risk frameworks connecting portfolio strategy to the bank’s overall risk appetite. Pricing discipline oriented toward long-term capital stewardship rather than short-term P&L.

The Results

Annual intermediation economics~USD 218M managed
Risk managementConsistent risk-adjusted returns within institutional parameters
Market conditions navigatedSovereign rating transitions, currency volatility, structural liquidity constraints

What I Learned

Capital allocation is where financial analysis meets institutional judgment. The discipline to decline an exposure is often as important as the ability to identify one. This experience shaped how I approach resource allocation at enterprise level.

LAFISE Valores · 2005–2013

From Last Place to Profitability in Twelve Months

2005–2013 · LAFISE Valores · Analyst to Interim CEO

The Institutional Challenge

Appointed to lead the turnaround of a brokerage ranked last in profitability. The firm had lost credibility, operational discipline had weakened, and direction was unclear. In a concentrated market, last place accelerates client and talent attrition.

The Hard Decisions

With limited resources, I had to rebuild credibility, restructure costs, and restore discipline while retaining key expertise. Priorities constantly competed: short-term revenue versus structural fixes, growth versus profitability. I consistently chose the latter.

What I Built

A simplified operating model focused on profitability and reliability. Clear accountability for financial outcomes at every level.

The Results

Starting positionLast place in industry profitability
ROE-11.5% → 7.3% (+1,900 b.p.)
TrajectoryAnalyst to interim CEO over progressive roles

What I Learned

Under constraint, discipline matters more than ambition — and credibility, once lost, compounds slowly when it begins to recover. That early lesson has shaped every subsequent leadership decision.
